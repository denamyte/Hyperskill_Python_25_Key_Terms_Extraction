Theory

In this stage, we will take a closer look at the TF-IDF approach. You can use it to calculate the importance of a word in a document or in a text collection. It tries to highlight the words that are more interesting, or more frequent in one specific document, not across documents. Search engines use this measure to score and rank the documents according to a user's query. It is also frequently used for text classification and text summarization, fundamental NLP tasks. The method is not computationally expensive and yields excellent results for such tasks.

For this project, we need to calculate the TF-IDF for every word in all news stories!

Use TfidfVectorizer from sklearn to solve this task. You may also find the function toarray() useful. It converts a sparse matrix that sklearn uses to an ordinary n-dimensional array, so, using indexation, you may access the terms and their scores in a more familiar way. Have a look at the Machine Learning Master tutorial on how to encode the text data for machine learning for details.

In the end, output five words with the highest TF-IDF score for each document. A higher metric score will indicate that these words have more importance and specific to this particular document, not for others.

Description

In this stage, you will get familiar with some theories behind this method and work with sklearn, one of the most popular Python machine learning libraries.

Take a look at the attached news file. Please use it in your project.

Don't forget to POS-annotate the whole news article at once, not word by word. This means that when you count the TF-IDF metric, you need to calculate it on the collection of news documents that consist of nouns only.
Objectives

In this stage, your program should:

  1. Read an XML-file containing stories and headlines.
  2. Extract the headers and the text.
  3. Tokenize each text.
  4. Exclude the tokens that start with ', stopwords, and punctuation marks.
  5. Lemmatize the remaining tokens in the story. Take into consideration only those lemmas, the length of which is longer than 1.
  6. Count the TF-IDF metric for each word in all stories. In other words, apply it to the entire collection of news documents.
  7. Pick the five best scoring words.
  8. Print each story's headline and the five most frequent words in descending order. Take a look at the sample output below. Display the titles and keywords in the same order they are presented in the file.
